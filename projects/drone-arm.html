Topology Optimized Drone Arm

Mechanical Engineer · Computational Physics and Mechanics Lab (CPML) · 2024–Present

I designed, optimized, and experimentally tested a topology-optimized quadcopter arm to replace traditional carbon-fiber drone arms—which account for over 68% of total structural weight. The goal was to drastically reduce mass while improving stiffness and maintaining structural integrity under flight loads.

Using a commonly used consumer quadcopter arm as the design domain, I removed unnecessary geometry, defined mounting and keep-out regions, and prepared the part for optimization. I selected nTopology for its convergence behavior and topology workflow flexibility compared to SolidWorks or ANSYS, and applied multiple load cases to represent thrust and maneuver loads.

The final optimized geometry was manufactured via Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) using PLA for rapid prototyping. I built a custom test stand and performed static load testing to validate simulation results against measured deformation.

The topology-optimized structure demonstrated a significantly improved stiffness-to-weight ratio and reduced stress concentrations relative to the baseline arm, while remaining fully compatible with the original quadcopter hardware.

Problem & Goals

The baseline drone arm exhibited excessive deflection and high stress under representative flight loads. The goals for the replacement arm were to:

  • Reduce tip deflection under thrust and maneuver loads by roughly an order of magnitude,
  • Lower maximum von Mises stress while preserving safety factors,
  • Remain compatible with the existing drone architecture and mounting pattern, and
  • Be manufacturable via low-cost additive processes with short lead time.

Baseline vs Optimized Structural Response

I first characterized the baseline arm under an equivalent thrust load, then used nTopology to create a topology-optimized design within the same mounting envelope. The optimized arm dramatically lowered peak stress and deflection while also reducing mass.

Optimization Model, Mesh & Boundary Conditions

The topology optimization problem was formulated on a meshed design domain that captured the full arm envelope while enforcing passive regions around the motor mount and fuselage bracket. Boundary conditions and loads were defined to match the dominant bending case seen in flight.

Manufacturing & Experimental Validation

The final arm was printed in PLA using FDM to enable fast iteration. I built a simple fixture and test setup to apply known loads and measure deflection at the tip, then compared experimental results against finite element predictions from nTopology.

Impact

This project demonstrated how topology optimization and additive manufacturing can deliver major improvements in stiffness and stress performance while keeping designs practical to fabricate. The work was presented at the Vanderbilt Undergraduate Research Conference with support from VUSE and SURP, and provides a template for future structural redesigns on the same drone platform.
