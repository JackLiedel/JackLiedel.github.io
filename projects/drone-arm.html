Topology Optimized Drone Arm

Mechanical Engineer · Computational Physics and Mechanics Lab (CPML) · 2024–Present

I designed, optimized, and experimentally tested a topology-optimized quadcopter arm to replace traditional carbon-fiber drone arms—which account for over 68% of total structural weight. The goal was to drastically reduce mass while improving stiffness and maintaining structural integrity under flight loads.

Using a commonly used consumer quadcopter arm as the design domain, I removed unnecessary geometry and prepared the part for optimization. I selected nTopology for its superior convergence behavior and topology workflow flexibility compared to tools such as SolidWorks or ANSYS. Multiple load cases were applied to match real-world thrust and maneuver loads.

The final optimized geometry was manufactured via Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) using PLA, which offered the best strength, impact resistance, and ease of printing for rapid prototyping. I fabricated a custom test stand and performed static load testing to validate simulation results against physical deformation.

This topology-optimized structure demonstrated significantly improved structural efficiency, offering higher stiffness-to-weight ratio and reduced stress concentrations compared to the baseline arm, while remaining fully compatible with existing quadcopter mounting interfaces.

Problem & Goals

The baseline drone arm exhibited excessive deflection and stress under flight loads. The goal was to design a replacement arm that:

  • Reduced tip deflection by an order of magnitude under representative loading,
  • Lowered maximum von Mises stress while maintaining safety margins,
  • Remained compatible with the existing drone architecture and mounting pattern, and
  • Could be manufactured quickly via additive processes at low cost.

Baseline vs Optimized Design

I rebuilt the baseline arm geometry, applied loading and constraints, and used nTopology to drive topology optimization. The workflow produced an arm that reduced deflection by ~70× and peak stress by ~6.6× compared to the original design while also simplifying manufacturing.

Optimization Workflow & Analysis

I set up load cases based on thrust, weight, and maneuver-induced loads, then iterated on topology optimization parameters to balance stiffness, mass, and printability. Post-processing included smoothing, filleting critical regions, and checking manufacturability.

Manufacturing & Experimental Validation

The final design was fabricated using additive manufacturing. I built fixtures and a test setup to measure stiffness and compare experimental results against simulation, validating the large reduction in deflection and stress.

Impact

This work demonstrated that topology optimization combined with additive manufacturing can deliver dramatic improvements in stiffness and stress while keeping designs practical to build. I presented the project at the Vanderbilt Undergraduate Research Conference with support from VUSE and SURP.
